FIG. 1. Living wage information by county. Living wage is an estimate for the cost of living in that location. Data is taken from the Living Wage calculator [1] and historical snapshots of the website. Hovering over a county displays the living wage value as well as the poverty wage and the minimum wage for that county. Missing data is indicated by a gray fill.

According to the Living Wage calculator [1], the living wage model "is a market-based approach that draws upon geographically specific expenditure data related to a family's likely minimum food, childcare, health insurance, housing, transportation, and other basic necessities (e.g. clothing, personal care items, etc.) costs." This value depends on the number of earners and children, so we assume one adult worker with zero children--as most graduate students fit into this category. The map in Fig. 1 displays wage data for this group by county over nine years. For practically every single county, the minimum wage is well below the living wage and is in fact much closer to the poverty wage. Furthermore, while the living wage has almost doubled in many counties over the nine years of data, the minimum wage has not been increased to match. These are concerning issues but separate from the motivations of this visualization.

FIG. 2. Average stipend amount for graduate students. Data is taken from Ph.D. Stipends [2], a site where graduate students can self report stipend information. Hovering over a datapoint displays the average stipend amount and the living wage surplus/deficit.

So now that we know it is expensive to live in the U.S., how well do graduate stipends support their students when compared to the cost of living? In Fig. 2 we provide evidence supporting the claim that, in general, graduate students are not getting paid enough. Each circle in the map above represents a single university. A circle's size corresponds to the stipend amount for that university averaged over all entries. As such, we lose information about the pay of specific programs. A circle's color indicates whether that university is paying more/less than the living wage for the county in which it is located.

The trend to notice is that over the years, while stipends have increased, they have not kept up with the cost of living. This can be seen as more universities are being shaded in red--indicating a stipend that is below the living wage for that county.
